Technology in teaching

{% include "prepare/sidebar.html" %}

Technology will likely play a big role in both your teacher training and teaching career.

Schools will likely have their own policies when it comes to using technology in the classroom, but you may be expected to use or develop skills in the following.

Interactive learning environments

Many teachers use tools like interactive whiteboards and smart displays. You can use these to engage your students with multimedia presentations, interactive quizzes and get real-time feedback.

Using a smartboard, you can annotate directly on the screen and display videos.

Digital collaboration

Some schools use digital collaboration tools to enable students to work together on projects, share resources and communicate.

Often, digital platforms might be most used for setting homework.

Using artificial intelligence (AI)

AI can be used to generate lesson resources, support lesson planning and bring learning to life for pupils.

You can the Department for Education’s support materials on using AI in education to learn more.

Adaptive learning

Technology can support you to cater to each students’ individual needs by providing customised exercises and resources for different pupils.

This can be particularly useful for supporting pupils with special educational needs and disabilities (SEND).

Digital assessments

Many teachers use online assessment tools to make evaluations more engaging and interactive. These platforms offer instant feedback which helps students understand their mistakes and learn from them immediately.

You can also use data from these assessments to identify areas where students need more support.

Virtual and augmented reality

Virtual and augmented reality allows you to take students on virtual trips outside of the classroom and bring topics to life.

It provides immersive learning experiences that can make complex subjects more understandable and exciting.

Digital literacy

As a teacher, you’ll likely be expected to ensure students are digitally literate. This means teaching them how to use technology responsibly, understand online safety and develop critical thinking skills when using digital content.

You could look at lessons and activities that help students build essential digital habits and skills from Common Sense (opens in new tab). They’re a non-profit who provide digital learning tools.
